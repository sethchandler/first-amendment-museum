I. THE RELIGION CLAUSES

The First Amendment provides: "Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof." Both clauses are incorporated against the states through the Fourteenth Amendment.

A. The Establishment Clause

1. Incorporation

The Establishment Clause applies to the states through the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. Everson v. Board of Education (1947)

2. Analytical Frameworks

a. The Lemon Test (Historically Dominant)
A government action violates the Establishment Clause if it (1) lacks a secular legislative purpose, (2) has a primary effect that advances or inhibits religion, or (3) fosters excessive government entanglement with religion. Lemon v. Kurtzman (1971)

Note: The Court has acknowledged Lemon's shortcomings over the decades and has increasingly declined to apply it.

b. The Endorsement Test
Government action violates the Establishment Clause if a reasonable observer would perceive the government as endorsing or disapproving of religion. Lynch v. Donnelly (1984); County of Allegheny v. ACLU (1989)
c. The Coercion Test
Government may not coerce anyone to support or participate in religion or its exercise. In the public-school context, even subtle and indirect coercion is impermissible. Lee v. Weisman (1992)
d. The Historical Practices and Understandings Test (Current Standard)
The Establishment Clause must be interpreted by reference to historical practices and understandings. The Court has abandoned Lemon and its endorsement-test offshoot. Kennedy v. Bremerton School District (2022); American Legion v. American Humanist Ass'n (2019)

3. Religious Displays on Public Property

A longstanding religious monument or display on public land does not necessarily violate the Establishment Clause, particularly where it has acquired secular meaning through the passage of time. American Legion v. American Humanist Ass'n (2019)
Context matters. A Ten Commandments display among other historical monuments is permissible, but a display whose context reveals a predominantly religious purpose violates the Establishment Clause. Van Orden v. Perry (2005); McCreary County v. ACLU of Kentucky (2005)
A crèche displayed alone in a government building conveys an impermissible endorsement of religion. A Christmas display combining a menorah, Christmas tree, and sign saluting liberty does not. County of Allegheny v. ACLU (1989)

4. Government Discrimination Among Religions

Legislative prayer does not violate the Establishment Clause where it is consistent with the long tradition of opening legislative sessions with prayer. A legislature need not censor prayer content, but may not coerce participation. Town of Greece v. Galloway (2014); Marsh v. Chambers (1983)

5. Financial Benefits to Religious Entities

a. Aid to Colleges and Hospitals
Government financial aid to religiously affiliated colleges and hospitals is generally permissible where the aid is secular in nature and the institutions are not pervasively sectarian. Tilton v. Richardson (1971)
b. Aid to K–12 Schools
A neutral government program that provides aid to a broad class of citizens, who in turn direct that aid to religious schools through independent private choices, does not violate the Establishment Clause. Zelman v. Simmons-Harris (2002)
Government may provide secular textbooks and instructional materials to religious schools on a neutral basis. Mitchell v. Helms (2000); Board of Education v. Allen (1968)
If a state provides public funds for private education, the Free Exercise Clause forbids excluding religious schools from the program solely because they are religious (status) or because they teach religion (use). Espinoza v. Montana Dept. of Revenue (2020); Carson v. Makin (2022)

6. Prohibition on Government Discrimination Against Religion in Funding

Where the government provides a generally available public benefit, it may not exclude an otherwise eligible recipient solely because it is a religious organization. Trinity Lutheran Church of Columbia, Inc. v. Comer (2017)

7. Tax Exemptions

A property-tax exemption for religious organizations does not violate the Establishment Clause where it is granted as part of a broader class of exemptions for nonprofit organizations. Walz v. Tax Comm'n of City of New York (1970)

8. Curriculum Controls

A state may not prohibit the teaching of evolution or require the teaching of "creation science" alongside evolution, because such requirements have an impermissible religious purpose. Edwards v. Aguillard (1987); Epperson v. Arkansas (1968)

9. Religious Activities in Public Schools

State-sponsored prayer in public schools is unconstitutional, even if participation is nominally voluntary. Engel v. Vitale (1962)
Mandatory Bible readings and recitation of the Lord's Prayer in public schools violate the Establishment Clause. Abington School Dist. v. Schempp (1963)
A state statute authorizing a moment of silence "for meditation or voluntary prayer" violates the Establishment Clause if the legislative history reveals a religious purpose. Wallace v. Jaffree (1985)
Student-led, student-initiated prayer at a school football game violates the Establishment Clause because it bears the imprint of state action. Santa Fe Independent School Dist. v. Doe (2000)
Government-directed prayer at a public school graduation ceremony is unconstitutional due to the coercive pressure it places on students. Lee v. Weisman (1992)

10. Accommodations for Religious Students and Equal Access

A public school that makes its facilities available to noncurricular student groups must provide equal access to religious student groups. Exclusion constitutes viewpoint discrimination. Good News Club v. Milford Central School (2001); Widmar v. Vincent (1981)
A public university may condition official recognition on an "all-comers" policy requiring student groups to accept all students. Christian Legal Society v. Martinez (2010)

B. The Free Exercise Clause

1. Incorporation

The Free Exercise Clause applies to the states through the Fourteenth Amendment. Cantwell v. Connecticut (1940)

2. Meaning of "Religious Belief"

The Free Exercise Clause protects sincere religious beliefs; courts may not evaluate the truth of religious claims but may assess sincerity. The belief need not be part of an organized religion. Thomas v. Review Board (1981); United States v. Seeger (1965); Frazee v. Illinois Dept. of Employment Security (1989)

3. The Smith Rule: Neutral, Generally Applicable Laws

A neutral law of general applicability that incidentally burdens religious exercise does not violate the Free Exercise Clause and need not be justified by a compelling governmental interest. Employment Division v. Smith (1990)

4. Exceptions to Smith

a. Non-neutral or Non-generally-Applicable Laws
A law that targets religious conduct must satisfy strict scrutiny. Church of the Lukumi Babalu Aye, Inc. v. City of Hialeah (1993)
A law is not generally applicable if it provides a mechanism for individualized exemptions but denies comparable exemptions for religious exercise. Fulton v. Philadelphia (2021)
A law is not neutral and generally applicable if it treats any comparable secular activity more favorably than religious exercise. Tandon v. Newsom (2021)
b. The Sherbert Compelling-Interest Test (Pre-Smith / RFRA Context)
A law that substantially burdens a sincerely held religious belief must be justified by a compelling governmental interest and use the least restrictive means. Sherbert v. Verner (1963); Wisconsin v. Yoder (1972)
States could not deny unemployment benefits to individuals who lost their jobs because of Sabbath observance or sincerely held religious convictions. Sherbert v. Verner (1963); Thomas v. Review Board (1981); Hobbie v. Unemployment Appeals Comm'n (1987)

5. Exemptions from Antidiscrimination Laws

Government cannot apply antidiscrimination laws in a manner that exhibits hostility toward religion. Masterpiece Cakeshop, Ltd. v. Colorado Civil Rights Comm'n (2018)
The Free Speech Clause prohibits a state from compelling an individual to create expressive works that convey a message with which the creator disagrees, even under a public-accommodations law. 303 Creative LLC v. Elenis (2023)

6. RFRA and RLUIPA

RFRA exceeded Congress's Section 5 power as applied to the states. City of Boerne v. Flores (1997)
RFRA remains valid as applied to the federal government and requires strict scrutiny of substantial burdens on religious exercise. Gonzales v. O Centro Espírita Beneficente União do Vegetal (2006); Burwell v. Hobby Lobby Stores, Inc. (2014)

II. THE FREE-SPEECH CLAUSE

A. Content-Based Regulation of Protected Expression

1. The Presumption Against Content-Based Laws

Content-based laws are presumptively unconstitutional and must satisfy strict scrutiny. Reed v. Town of Gilbert (2015)
A law is content-based if it draws distinctions based on the message a speaker conveys. Reed v. Town of Gilbert (2015); Barr v. American Ass'n of Political Consultants, Inc. (2020)

2. Viewpoint Discrimination

Viewpoint discrimination is an egregious form of content discrimination. The government may not suppress speech based on the speaker's ideology, opinion, or perspective. Rosenberger v. Rector & Visitors of Univ. of Virginia (1995); R.A.V. v. City of St. Paul (1992)

3. Strict Scrutiny Application

Content-based restrictions rarely survive strict scrutiny. Reed v. Town of Gilbert (2015)

4. Content-Based vs. Content-Neutral: The Dividing Line

A facially content-neutral law that cannot be justified without reference to the content of the regulated speech is content-based. Reed v. Town of Gilbert (2015)
A law distinguishing between on-premises and off-premises signs does not require strict scrutiny if the distinction is based on location rather than communicative content. City of Austin v. Reagan National Advertising of Austin, LLC (2022)

B. Content-Neutral Regulation and Forum Designations

1. Content-Neutral Restrictions

Content-neutral regulations are subject to intermediate scrutiny: narrowly tailored to serve a significant governmental interest with ample alternative channels of communication. "Narrowly tailored" does not mean least restrictive means. Ward v. Rock Against Racism (1989); Clark v. Community for Creative Non-Violence (1984)

2. Forum Analysis

a. Traditional Public Forum
Streets, sidewalks, and parks are traditional public forums. Content-based restrictions require strict scrutiny; content-neutral TPM restrictions require intermediate scrutiny. Perry Education Ass'n v. Perry Local Educators' Ass'n (1983); United States v. Grace (1983)
b. Designated (Limited) Public Forum
When the government opens a nontraditional forum for discourse, it is bound by the same standards within the scope of the designation. Restrictions must be reasonable and viewpoint-neutral. Perry Education Ass'n v. Perry Local Educators' Ass'n (1983); Cornelius v. NAACP Legal Defense & Educational Fund, Inc. (1985); Rosenberger v. Rector & Visitors of Univ. of Virginia (1995)
c. Nonpublic Forum
Restrictions need only be reasonable and not based on viewpoint. International Society for Krishna Consciousness, Inc. v. Lee (1992); Perry Education Ass'n v. Perry Local Educators' Ass'n (1983)

3. Secondary Effects Doctrine

Zoning of adult entertainment businesses may be treated as content-neutral if designed to combat secondary effects (crime, decreased property values). Subject to intermediate scrutiny. City of Renton v. Playtime Theatres, Inc. (1986)

C. Regulation of Expressive Conduct

1. The Spence Test: When Conduct Is "Speech"

Conduct triggers First Amendment protection where (1) the actor intends to convey a particularized message, and (2) the message would likely be understood by viewers. Spence v. Washington (1974); Texas v. Johnson (1989)

2. The O'Brien Test

A regulation of expressive conduct is constitutional if (1) within government's power, (2) furthers an important interest, (3) unrelated to suppression of expression, and (4) the incidental restriction is no greater than essential. United States v. O'Brien (1968)

3. Flag Burning

Flag burning is protected expressive conduct. The government may not prohibit expression of an idea simply because society finds it disagreeable. Texas v. Johnson (1989); United States v. Eichman (1990)

4. Other Symbolic Speech

Wearing black armbands in school as political protest is protected symbolic speech. Tinker v. Des Moines Independent Community School District (1969)
Nude dancing is marginally protected but may be regulated under O'Brien. Barnes v. Glen Theatre, Inc. (1991); City of Erie v. Pap's A.M. (2000)
Conduct that is not inherently expressive (e.g., accommodating military recruiters) is not protected. Rumsfeld v. Forum for Academic and Institutional Rights, Inc. (2006)

D. Regulation of Unprotected Expression

Certain categories of speech receive no First Amendment protection. The government may not create new categories by cost-benefit analysis.

1. Fighting Words

"Fighting words"—personally abusive words inherently likely to provoke a violent reaction—are unprotected. Chaplinsky v. New Hampshire (1942)
Even within unprotected categories, the government may not impose viewpoint-based distinctions. R.A.V. v. City of St. Paul (1992)
The doctrine has been significantly narrowed since Chaplinsky. Gooding v. Wilson (1972); Cohen v. California (1971)

2. True Threats

"True threats" encompass statements where the speaker means to communicate a serious expression of intent to commit unlawful violence. Virginia v. Black (2003)
The government must show the defendant had some subjective understanding of the threatening nature—at minimum, recklessness. Counterman v. Colorado (2023)

3. Incitement

Speech advocating illegal conduct is protected unless (1) directed to inciting imminent lawless action and (2) likely to produce such action. Brandenburg v. Ohio (1969)
Advocacy of future illegal activity is protected. Hess v. Indiana (1973)

4. Obscenity

The Miller test: (1) appeals to the prurient interest applying community standards, (2) depicts sexual conduct in a patently offensive way, (3) lacks serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value. Miller v. California (1973)
The "serious value" prong is judged by a reasonable-person standard, not community standards. Pope v. Illinois (1987)
Virtual child pornography (no actual minors) is protected unless it independently satisfies Miller. Ashcroft v. Free Speech Coalition (2002)
Actual child pornography is unprotected regardless of whether it meets Miller. New York v. Ferber (1982)

5. Defamation

A public official must prove "actual malice"—knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard for truth—by convincing clarity. New York Times Co. v. Sullivan (1964)
The actual-malice standard extends to public figures. Curtis Publishing Co. v. Butts (1967)
States may not impose liability on private-figure defamation without at least a showing of fault (negligence). No presumed or punitive damages without actual malice. Gertz v. Robert Welch, Inc. (1974)
In suits by private plaintiffs on matters of public concern, the plaintiff bears the burden of proving falsity. Philadelphia Newspapers, Inc. v. Hepps (1986)
For speech on purely private concern, states may permit presumed and punitive damages without actual malice. Dun & Bradstreet, Inc. v. Greenmoss Builders, Inc. (1985)

E. Regulation of Commercial Speech

Commercial speech—expression proposing a commercial transaction—is protected but receives lesser protection. Virginia State Board of Pharmacy v. Virginia Citizens Consumer Council, Inc. (1976)
The Central Hudson test: (1) lawful and not misleading? If so: (2) substantial governmental interest? (3) regulation directly advances interest? (4) not more extensive than necessary? "Not more extensive" means reasonable fit, not least restrictive means. Central Hudson Gas & Electric Corp. v. Public Service Comm'n (1980); Board of Trustees of State Univ. of N.Y. v. Fox (1989)
A complete ban on truthful commercial speech about lawful activity is difficult to justify. 44 Liquormart, Inc. v. Rhode Island (1996)

F. Regulation of Public School Students and Public Employees

1. Public Employee Speech

a. The Garcetti/Pickering Framework
When public employees speak pursuant to their official duties, they are not speaking as citizens for First Amendment purposes. Garcetti v. Ceballos (2006)
When a public employee speaks as a citizen on a matter of public concern, courts must balance the employee's interest against the employer's efficiency interest. Pickering v. Board of Education (1968)
If the speech addresses only personal interest, no First Amendment cause of action. Connick v. Myers (1983)
Sworn testimony by a public employee is citizen speech on a matter of public concern and is protected. Lane v. Franks (2014)
b. Government Employees' Political Activities
The Hatch Act's restrictions on partisan political activities by federal employees are constitutional. United States Civil Service Comm'n v. National Ass'n of Letter Carriers (1973); Broadrick v. Oklahoma (1973)
c. Patronage
The government may not condition public employment on political allegiance for non-policymaking positions. Elrod v. Burns (1976); Branti v. Finkel (1980); Rutan v. Republican Party of Illinois (1990)

2. Public School Student Speech

Students do not "shed their constitutional rights at the schoolhouse gate." Speech may be suppressed only if it would cause material and substantial disruption. Tinker v. Des Moines Independent Community School District (1969)
Schools may punish lewd, vulgar, or offensive speech at school events. Bethel School Dist. No. 403 v. Fraser (1986)
Schools may exercise editorial control over school-sponsored speech if reasonably related to legitimate pedagogical concerns. Hazelwood School Dist. v. Kuhlmeier (1988)
Schools may restrict speech reasonably viewed as promoting illegal drug use. Morse v. Frederick (2007)
Tinker extends to some off-campus speech, but schools' regulatory interests are diminished off campus. Mahanoy Area School Dist. v. B.L. (2021)

3. The Unconstitutional Conditions Doctrine

The government may not deny a benefit on a basis that infringes constitutionally protected speech. Perry v. Sindermann (1972); Speiser v. Randall (1958)
When the government is itself the speaker, it may choose the message without implicating unconstitutional conditions. Rust v. Sullivan (1991); Walker v. Texas Division, Sons of Confederate Veterans, Inc. (2015)

4. Government Speech and Compelled Speech

The Free Speech Clause does not regulate government speech. The government may select its own messages. Walker v. Texas Division, Sons of Confederate Veterans, Inc. (2015); Pleasant Grove City v. Summum (2009)
The government may not compel individuals to express messages with which they disagree. West Virginia State Board of Education v. Barnette (1943)
A state may not require a newspaper to publish reply material. Miami Herald Publishing Co. v. Tornillo (1974)
The government may not compel a private business to create expressive works conveying messages it opposes. 303 Creative LLC v. Elenis (2023)

G. Prior Restraint, Vagueness, and Overbreadth

1. Prior Restraints

Prior restraints bear a heavy presumption against constitutional validity. Near v. Minnesota ex rel. Olson (1931)
Even national-security interests face an extremely heavy burden to justify prior restraint. New York Times Co. v. United States (1971)
A gag order on the press is a prior restraint permissible only as a last resort. Nebraska Press Ass'n v. Stuart (1976)

2. Licensing and Permit Systems

Licensing schemes must contain narrow, objective, and definite standards. Standardless schemes are unconstitutional prior restraints. Forsyth County v. Nationalist Movement (1992)
Content-neutral permit schemes need not satisfy Freedman's procedural safeguards. Thomas v. Chicago Park District (2002)

3. Procedural Safeguards for Content-Based Licensing

Content-based licensing must provide: (1) burden of proof on the censor, (2) prompt decision, (3) prompt judicial review. Freedman v. Maryland (1965)

4. Vagueness

A law is unconstitutionally vague if it fails to provide fair notice and lacks standards to prevent arbitrary enforcement. Standards are especially demanding for speech restrictions. Hoffman Estates v. Flipside, Hoffman Estates, Inc. (1982)

5. Overbreadth

A statute is facially overbroad if it prohibits a substantial amount of protected speech relative to its legitimate sweep. Broadrick v. Oklahoma (1973); United States v. Williams (2008); Virginia v. Hicks (2003)

III. FREEDOM OF THE PRESS

1. Publication of Truthful Information

If a newspaper lawfully obtains truthful information about a matter of public significance, state officials may not punish publication absent a need of the highest order. Smith v. Daily Mail Publishing Co. (1979); Florida Star v. B.J.F. (1989); Cox Broadcasting Corp. v. Cohn (1975)

2. Press Access to Court Proceedings

The First Amendment guarantees the press and public a right of access to criminal trials. Richmond Newspapers, Inc. v. Virginia (1980)
The right extends to preliminary hearings, voir dire, and pretrial proceedings. Closure requires a compelling interest, narrowly tailored. Press-Enterprise Co. v. Superior Court (Press-Enterprise II) (1986); Press-Enterprise Co. v. Superior Court (Press-Enterprise I) (1984); Globe Newspaper Co. v. Superior Court (1982)

3. Protective Orders for Discovery Materials

Courts may restrict dissemination of information obtained through civil discovery without violating the First Amendment. Seattle Times Co. v. Rhinehart (1984)

4. Press Access to Prisons

The press has no constitutional right of access to prisons beyond that afforded to the general public. Pell v. Procunier (1974); Houchins v. KQED, Inc. (1978)

5. Reporter's Privilege

The First Amendment does not provide reporters with an absolute privilege against appearing before a grand jury or disclosing confidential sources. Branzburg v. Hayes (1972)

6. Press Not Exempt from Generally Applicable Laws

The press is not exempt from general laws that do not single out the press. Cohen v. Cowles Media Co. (1991)

IV. FREEDOM OF ASSOCIATION

1. Expressive Association

The First Amendment protects the right to associate for expressive purposes, including a right not to associate. Roberts v. United States Jaycees (1984); Boy Scouts of America v. Dale (2000)
An organization may resist forced inclusion of an unwanted person if that person's presence would significantly affect the group's ability to advocate its viewpoint. Boy Scouts of America v. Dale (2000); Hurley v. Irish-American Gay, Lesbian and Bisexual Group of Boston, Inc. (1995)

2. Intimate Association

The Constitution protects certain intimate relationships. Not every private club qualifies. Roberts v. United States Jaycees (1984); City of Dallas v. Stanglin (1989)

3. Freedom of Association and the Electoral Process

a. Campaign Contributions
Contribution limits are constitutional. Subject to "closely drawn" scrutiny. Buckley v. Valeo (1976); Nixon v. Shrink Missouri Government PAC (2000)
b. Campaign Expenditures
Independent expenditure limits are unconstitutional. Extended to corporations and unions. Buckley v. Valeo (1976); Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission (2010)
c. Corporate Political Speech
Political speech does not lose protection because its source is a corporation. Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission (2010)
d. Ballot Access
Severe ballot-access restrictions trigger strict scrutiny; reasonable, nondiscriminatory restrictions receive flexible review. Anderson v. Celebrezze (1983); Burdick v. Takushi (1992); Timmons v. Twin Cities Area New Party (1997)
e. Party Regulation
A blanket primary that forces a party to associate with hostile voters is unconstitutional. But a nonpartisan "top two" primary may survive a facial challenge. California Democratic Party v. Jones (2000); Tashjian v. Republican Party of Connecticut (1986); Washington State Grange v. Washington State Republican Party (2008)

4. Bar Membership

The government may not condition bar membership on disclosure of political associations unrelated to fitness to practice law. Baird v. State Bar (1971); In re Stolar (1971)

5. Laws Prohibiting or Punishing Membership

Mere membership in an organization cannot be punished absent specific intent to further unlawful aims. Scales v. United States (1961); Noto v. United States (1961); Elfbrandt v. Russell (1966)
Government employees may not be discharged solely for party membership. Elrod v. Burns (1976); Branti v. Finkel (1980)
